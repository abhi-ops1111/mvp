Introduction to Calculus: Derivatives

Estimated read time: 8 min · Topic: Mathematics

What is a Derivative?

In differential calculus, the derivative of a function of a real variable measures the sensitivity to change of the function value (output value) with respect to a change in its argument (input value). Formally, the derivative is defined as the limit of the difference quotient as the interval approaches zero: f'(x) = lim[h→0] (f(x+h) − f(x)) / h. The process of finding a derivative is called differentiation. The reverse operation of differentiation is called anti-differentiation or integration. Derivatives are fundamental to the solutions of problems in calculus and differential equations, and have widespread application in physics, engineering, economics, and biology to model rates of change such as velocity and acceleration. The notation f'(x), dy/dx, and Df(x) are all used interchangeably in the literature.

The Chain Rule

The chain rule is a formula for computing the derivative of the composition of two or more functions. If f and g are functions such that h(x) = f(g(x)), the chain rule states that h'(x) = f'(g(x)) · g'(x). In Leibniz notation, if y = f(u) and u = g(x), then dy/dx = (dy/du) · (du/dx). This rule is essential when differentiating composite functions and is one of the most powerful tools in differential calculus. The chain rule generalises to multiple compositions by iterative application and can be extended to multivariable calculus via partial derivatives and the Jacobian matrix. Notably, the chain rule underpins the backpropagation algorithm in neural networks, making it central to modern machine learning.

Integration as the Inverse

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus establishes the relationship between differentiation and integration, two central operations in calculus. The theorem has two parts: the first part states that if F is an antiderivative of f on an interval [a,b], then ∫[a to b] f(x) dx = F(b) − F(a). The second part states that the derivative of an integral with variable upper bound is the original integrand: d/dx ∫[a to x] f(t) dt = f(x). This duality means integration can be used to compute areas under curves, total accumulation, arc lengths, volumes of solids of revolution, and probability distributions in statistics. Riemann sums provide the foundational construction but Lebesgue integration offers a more powerful generalisation.
